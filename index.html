June's dispute mix is dominated by an ATO event on physical cards in Mexico (Tutuka): 67.5% of 274 disputes remain in progress, so $33,094 is still at risk, not yet lost.

Disputes opened fell in absolute terms from 579 in May to 274, but the composition shifted decisively toward fraud driven by the ATO on physical cards in Mexico (Tutuka). Physical-card share climbed from 47.3% in April and 86.4% in May to 77.2% in June, while 'transaction not recognised' now accounts for 92.6% of cases versus its structurally high but lower prior levels. Processor concentration reinforces this: tutukaBrazil (48.6%) and tutuka (42.0%) together hold roughly 90.6% of volume. These signals converge on a single compromise event rather than dispersed merchant issues. The operational consequence is a heavy near-term case-handling load concentrated on one processor family.
The 67.5% in-progress share is the highest of the three months, up from 203 cases in May and just 45 in April. This is expected, not alarming: the network resolution window runs 45–120 days and June's ATO-driven cases are recent, so outcomes simply have not landed yet. The 3.3% win rate and $0 lost reflect this timing, not poor performance — nothing has been ruled against us. As this cohort matures over the next one to two months, wins and merchant refunds should convert. The real read on recovery quality will only be visible once these 185 cases resolve.

Only $94 has been recovered from wins so far — again a timing artefact, since June's ATO cohort has not yet cleared the network. Compared with May ($22,405 to be recovered) and April ($8,277), the at-risk pool has grown materially as fraud volume concentrated. The consequence: a large provisional exposure whose final split between recovery and loss depends entirely on how the Tutuka fraud cohort resolves. Confirmed loss should be read against prior clean months, where loss was $655 (May) and $1,307 (April).
Physical cards jumped to 77.2% of volume, and while that is below May's 86.4%, it represents a full reversal from April, where virtual cards led at 52.7% and physical was only 47.3%. This physical dominance is consistent with an ATO targeting issued plastic in Mexico on the Tutuka rails. The 3DS distribution supports a fraud reading: 37.3% of cases carry 'No signal' and only 1.7% show authenticated or 3DS-attempted flows, indicating card-present or unauthenticated channels where step-up authentication did not intervene. The operational implication is that fraud controls for physical-card usage in the affected corridor warrant tightening, since these transactions bypassed authentication before surfacing as disputes.
Tutuka BPS eased to 5.60 from 6.06 in May, remaining above April's healthy 3.49 but comfortably under the 8 bps processor-review threshold. That the ratio did not spike despite the ATO-driven case surge suggests disputed amounts stayed proportionate to transaction volume. The top MCC, Computer Software Stores at 42.6% (up from its prior share), alongside Direct Marketing (10.2%) and Advertising Services (9.8%), points to fraud steered toward digital-goods and easily-monetised merchant categories typical of compromised-card cash-out. The consequence: BPS is currently safe, but a second wave of ATO disputes could push tutuka toward the 8 bps line and trigger processor scrutiny.
Fraud lens: June reads clearly as a fraud-concentrated month: the surge in physical cards (77.2%), the near-total dominance of tutuka and tutukaBrazil (~90.6% combined), and 'transaction not recognised' at 92.6% all converge on the Account Takeover event affecting physical cards in Mexico on the Tutuka processor. These are separate distributions, not a cross-tab, but their simultaneous movement is consistent with a single compromise driver rather than diffuse merchant disputes. The 3DS profile — 37.3% 'No signal' and only a fraction authenticated — is consistent with unauthenticated or card-present fraud slipping past step-up controls before being caught. Importantly, part of this elevated volume reflects Jeeves' own detection working: compromised transactions are being surfaced and disputed quickly, meaning higher dispute counts partly signal fraud being caught and acted on, not solely fraud rising. The absence of any losses so far and the large in-progress pool underscore that this is an active-response month, not a realized-loss month.
Watch: Track the 185 in-progress disputes as they clear the 45–120 day window; if the win-plus-refund rate on this ATO cohort settles below the ~64% blended recovery seen in April–May, escalate a review of the evidence being filed on Tutuka fraud cases. Monitor tutuka BPS monthly — a move from 5.60 toward the 8 bps threshold, especially if a second ATO wave lands, would risk processor review and warrants pre-emptive tightening of physical-card authentication in the Mexico corridor.

Section 8 · Glossary
Key terms explained
Chargeback
A forced reversal of a card transaction initiated by the card network on behalf of the cardholder. The merchant is debited and the issuer (Jeeves) recovers funds.
Win Rate
Percentage of filed disputes where the card network ruled in our favour. Calculated over opened disputes only — not over all tickets.
Temporary Credit
A provisional credit given to the client while the dispute is pending. Reversed if the dispute is lost; becomes permanent if won.
Financial Loss
A credit issued on a case that ended in a non-winning status (lost, not eligible, stopped, below $15). Confirmed cost to Jeeves.
BPS (Basis Points)
Disputed amount as a fraction of total transaction volume, × 10,000. E.g., 5 bps = 0.05% of volume disputed. Processors monitor this metric.
2nd Presentment
A second round of the dispute filed after reviewing the merchant's evidence rebuttal. Last chance before Pre-Arbitration.
Pre-Arbitration
An escalation stage where the card network acts as arbitrator. Higher fees apply. Used when both parties maintain their position after 2nd presentment.
3DS (3D Secure)
Authentication protocol (Verified by Visa / Mastercard SecureCode). If authenticated, fraud liability shifts to the merchant — making chargebacks very hard to file.
Not Eligible
Transaction cannot be disputed via the card network — often because it exceeded the 120-day dispute window or falls under a non-disputable transaction type.
MCC (Merchant Category Code)
4-digit code assigned by card networks to classify merchant type. E.g., 4121 = Taxicabs/Rideshare, 7372 = Software/SaaS. Used to spot dispute concentration by industry.
Stopped
Dispute was intentionally not continued after reviewing merchant evidence — typically when the evidence was compelling enough to justify stopping the process.
Refunded by Merchant
Merchant returned funds voluntarily (without a network ruling). Faster than a chargeback win and avoids dispute fees. Counted as a recovery.
